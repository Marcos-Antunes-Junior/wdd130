Content

Home page

I have good news! For the next years, you and I will live together in a single very specific point in the universe. More specifically, this point is the Earth Not just us but the Earth too, for the next years we will live together with some planets, moons and asteroids in a specific place called: Solar System. The solar system is defined by everything that is gravitationally bound to our sun (and it is our home also). It consists in the planets Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune; dwarf planets such as Pluto; dozens of moons; and millions of asteroids, comets, and meteoroids. But when did we discover that the Solar System is our house? In 1500 Nicolaus Copernicus discovered that the planets orbit the Sun. Only the Moon orbits the Earth. But he was afraid of people's reaction when he said this, so he didn't publish a full account of his ideas until 1543, the year of his death. So, Galileo Galilei pointed a telescope at the sky. He discovered moons orbiting Jupiter. He then verified that Copernicus might be right. It took 70 years to convince scientists that planets orbit the Sun. Currently, many people know what the Solar System is. That's it, this is our home. As residents of this wonderful place, we should meet our neighborhood! Please fasten your seat belts, put on your spacesuit and join me on this journey into the Solar System.

Planets

Each planet in the solar system has peculiarities so that they are classified according to their constitution. In this aspect we can highlight two types of planets: terrestrial planets (made up mainly of rocks), located closer to the sun such as Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars and gaseous planets (made up mostly of gases), which are larger in size and less dense than terrestrial ones: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. Planets are in constant motion so that they revolve around their own orbit or around the Sun. The rotation movement designates the movement that the planets perform around their own axis (equivalent to the time of 1 day). The translation movement defines the movement that the planets perform around the sun (equivalent to the time of 1 year). Are you enjoying this trip? Let us explore together the planets!

Sun, Moon and more

At the center of the Solar System is the Sun. He is a star, like the billions of other stars in the sky. The other stars look small because they are so far away from us. The Sun is important because it is what allows life on Earth, it provides the light, heat and energy. Without the sun, no life would exist on our planet. The diameter of the Sun is 1.392 million kilometers. It is mainly composed of hydrogen and helium, as well as iron, nickel, oxygen, silicon, carbon, nitrogen, sulfur, etc. Its temperature varies, reaching up to 5,505 degrees Celsius at the surface and 16 million degrees Celsius at the core. Fun fact: a trip from Earth to the Sun, in a spacecraft, would take about 124 days. Many of the planets have moons. The moon is a body that orbits a planet. Earth has a moon. Our Moon, orbits (moves around) the Earth. The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite. It is 384,400 km from the planet, and every year it moves 3.78 centimeters away. It has a thin and fragile exosphere, and its structure is composed of crust, mantle and core. Its surface is littered with impact craters, rock fragments and dust. The planet Mercury has no moon and neither does Venus. On the other hand, Jupiter has 63 moons.
